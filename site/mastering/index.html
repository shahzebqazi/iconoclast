Mastering

After the mix locks. Before the plant or the aggregator.

Where it sits

You hand off a mix that is supposed to be finished. Mastering is the pass that makes that mix survive phones, earbuds, club stacks, vinyl cutting, and whatever lossy codec sits in between—without turning the record into someone else’s template.

Mixing decides who wins the fight between guitars and vocals inside a song. Mastering pulls back to the release: spacing between tracks, whether the snare still reads when the limiter does its job, and whether track four accidentally nukes track five on a playlist because the low end drifted.

What you are hiring

A second monitoring path and a second opinion with EQ, dynamics, and clipping tools at the end of the line. Sometimes that is a half dB tilt and a limiter choice. Sometimes it is chasing a honk that only shows up after the bus comp printed. Sometimes the right move is telling you the vocal sibilance is baked in and a stem recall beats heroics on the stereo print.

Paperwork when you bring it: sequence gaps, ISRC embedding if your pipeline uses it, loudness and true-peak targets that match where the record is headed. If you do not know what you need yet, say where it is going (streaming-only, vinyl, both) and we map files to that.

Heavy music

Dense metal gets “safe” mastering fast: dull cymbals, smeared attacks, kicks that turn into carpet. The point here is translation with the aggression intact—riffs you can still parse when the stage is dirty.

Numbers and stem options live on Rates. File nerdery, loudness fights, and revision scope live on FAQ. When you are ready, Contact with refs and a deadline beats a vague DM.
